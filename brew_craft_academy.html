The Sensory World of Coffee

Sharpen your senses — they are the most powerful tools you own.

Our senses create depth and richness in our lives. We cannot turn them on and off, but we are not always consciously aware of them. When you develop sensory skills, you tell your brain that flavors, aromas, and textures matter.

Likes and dislikes are a valuable starting point, but once you expand your sensory vocabulary, you open yourself to a much richer experience. Anyone can become a coffee grader or sensory professional — the difference between your skills today and those of a professional taster is simply experience.

It's not about natural sensory skills, but rather the sensory awareness — or the passion to discover it. As our curiosity and ability to appreciate grow, so too does our enjoyment, because we become aware of truly what it is we love.

— Doug Langworthy, Global Tea Quality
The Four Steps of Coffee Tasting
1

Smell

Bring freshly poured coffee close to your nose and take a few sniffs of the aroma. What do you notice — floral, fruity, herbal, chocolaty? Begin here before a sip.

2

Slurp

Take a proper slurp, aspirating the coffee across your entire palate. This introduces air and spreads the coffee across all your taste receptors and into your retronasal passage.

3

Locate

Where on your palate do you notice sensations? Acidity on the sides? Bitterness at the back? Sweetness at the tip? Body throughout your mouth? Focus on one aspect at a time.

4

Describe

Find language for what you taste. Compare the flavor to things you've experienced. Is it like dark chocolate? Ripe citrus? Toasted nuts? Building this vocabulary is the work of a lifetime.

Building a Sensory Memory

As you continue to taste coffee, you begin to draw parallels between what you're tasting now and coffees you've tasted before. You may be reminded of a fruit you once ate or an aroma you once smelled. Two coffees may both be chocolaty, but one might have a more intense milk chocolate quality while the other is more like a cocoa nib.

As you grow as a taster, you will find deeper nuances of flavor. Your ability to match customers with their own ideal coffees will grow. Training your sensory skills leads to an enhanced level of understanding, respect, and appreciation for the world of coffee.

Unless you are experienced with actual individual flavor components, you will not be able to call them out when you taste coffee. Broadening your general taste experience will give you a foundation for understanding the nuances that are hints of flavor.

— Sergio Alvarez, Global Coffee Development

Chapter 2

Taste, Aroma & Flavor

Flavor is what our brains create when aroma and taste combine.
The Anatomy of Flavor

Although the words flavor and taste are used interchangeably in conversation, they are two different concepts. Taste is the sensation that happens in your mouth — primarily on your tongue. Flavor is how your brain synthesizes taste data with aroma data to form a complete experience.

You can test this yourself: pinch your nose and take a slurp of Pike Place® Roast. You'll detect sensations on your tongue, but the chocolaty and nutty flavors will be nowhere to be found. Release your nose — and suddenly the full flavor of cocoa and toasted nuts returns.

The Retronasal Passage
You can smell food from inside your mouth. The airway that connects your nasal and oral cavities — the retronasal passage — allows aromatic compounds to be "smelled" from the mouth. This retronasal olfaction is what makes the perception of flavor possible. It's why slurping coffee loudly (and aspirating it across your palate) is not rude — it's professional.
The Five Basic Tastes

Tap each taste to learn more about it and how it shows up in coffee.

🍯
Sweet
Indicates sugars are present — elementally pleasurable.
Sweetness signals energy (sugars like fructose, glucose, lactose). In coffee, the natural sweetness of well-roasted beans and quality milk comes from sugars that caramelize during roasting. A balanced, well-extracted espresso should taste smooth and sweet.
🍋
Sour
Caused by acids — citric, phosphoric, acetic.
Many different organic acids contribute to sour tastes. Citric acid (bright, citrus-like), phosphoric acid (tingly), and acetic acid (sharp, vinegar-like) all play roles in coffee acidity. Under-extracted coffee tastes sour — this is a key diagnosis tool for baristas.
☕
Bitter
Our brains evolved to detect toxins — but in small doses, it's fascinating.
A little bitterness makes many things interesting — dark chocolate, kale, coffee, and tea. Caffeine lends coffee some of its pleasant slight bitterness. Over-extracted coffee tastes bitter — the opposite of sour — so knowing the difference helps you diagnose brewing problems.
🧂
Salty
Primarily sodium and mineral salts.
In coffee, salinity comes from mineral content in the water used for brewing. The water chemistry in your store's filtration system is tuned to ensure the right mineral balance — enough to extract great flavor, but not so much as to taste overtly salty or mineral-heavy.
🥩
Umami
Japanese for "delicious taste" — rich, savory amino acids.
Umami is a substantial, rich, savory taste from amino acids in proteins. It can come from cooking, curing, or aging. In coffee, certain complex roasting compounds can contribute subtle savory or round, full qualities that add depth to the overall flavor profile — especially in aged coffees or certain origins.
The Tongue "Map" Myth
You may have seen a diagram showing sweet is tasted on the tip of the tongue and bitter on the back. This is a misunderstanding of a study published around 1901 — it was never intended to show where you tasted, but rather the different "thresholds for taste perception." In reality, all taste buds have a mixture of receptor cells and can perceive all five tastes, though some areas are slightly more sensitive to certain tastes than others.
Aroma: A Trillion Scents

Inside every nose are approximately six million olfactory receptors (a dog has 300 million, but has a harder time holding a cupping spoon). Through the instantaneous mapping of aromatic molecules to the olfactory bulb, humans can differentiate about a trillion different scents.

The aroma of coffee is made up of many different singular aromas. A Kenyan coffee might have a bouquet of grapefruit, raspberry, and lemon zest. A wet-hulled Indonesian coffee may feature aromatic notes of green herbs and molasses. One of the challenges of developing sensory skills is breaking down a complex bouquet into its singular scents.

The olfactory bulb is located close to the regions of the brain that process memory and emotions. This is why smell, more than any other sense, triggers powerful memories. The next time you taste a coffee and are reminded of Sunday mornings with your grandmother, congratulate your olfactory perceptions on their "scent-memory" capabilities.

The Art of Pairing

Food pairings can transform the experience of a coffee, completing it or even making it greater than the sum of its parts. Coffee flavors can be drawn out or enhanced when paired with certain foods.

🤝
Complementary Pairings
▾
The coffee and food enhance similar qualities in each other, and neither dominates. Example: pairing an herbal Sumatran coffee with a savory breakfast sandwich, or a bright Kenyan coffee with a lemon loaf cake. The flavors echo and amplify each other.
⚡
Contrasting Pairings
▾
Contrasting flavors combine to create a new experience. More challenging, but when successful the entire tasting experience is improved. Example: intense, roasty-sweet espresso with creamy vanilla ice cream. This pairing is about how together they form a surprising new taste — not echoing similarities.

Espresso at Our Heart

A small cup of concentrated magic — at the center of everything we make.

Espresso is a small cup of concentrated, aromatic, and intense coffee. As it brews, the machine's pressure forces very hot water to pass quickly through finely ground coffee. An extraordinarily unique brew, high-quality espresso is at the heart of many of our favorite handcrafted beverages.

Starbucks did not introduce espresso beverages until opening its sixth store in 1984. Although today espresso machines can be found most everywhere — in restaurants, cafés, even car dealerships — they are relative newcomers. The legendary history of coffee itself goes all the way back to the 9th century, but espresso is a modern innovation.

The Four Ms of Espresso

Brewing great espresso is like a well-choreographed dance. Tap each element to explore it.

M
The Coffee
Miscela (mee-SHAY-lah)
Quality espresso is only ever as good as the quality of the coffee itself. Every Starbucks® espresso uses high-quality arabica beans. Our ideal flavor profile for espresso is smooth and sweet. Not all coffees make great espresso — the high-pressure quick-brew process concentrates flavors, and sharper edges that are interesting in a brewed cup can be negatively amplified as espresso. Some coffees that are delightful as espresso alone may also lose their nuances when combined with steamed milk.
M
The Grinder
Macinazione (mah-cheenaht-see-OH-nay)
A high-quality burr grinder is especially critical for espresso due to the very small grind size required. Espresso grinders make micro-adjustments — the sharp burrs inside cut coffee beans into a consistent size immediately before brewing. Grinder maintenance matters: burrs dull like a kitchen knife, causing inconsistent grind size and off-flavors. Regular cleaning of the grinder protects equipment and upholds quality.
M
The Machine
Macchina (MAH-kee-nah)
Only an espresso machine can create the nine bars of pressure needed for true espresso. The earliest machines used a large lever; today a button activates an electric pump — but baristas still call it "pulling a shot." Modern machines set water temperature and brew pressure and guarantee consistency from the first beverage of the morning through peak rush. Starbucks uses machines from the super-automatic Mastrena® to the more hands-on Black Eagle.
M
The Barista
Mano (MAH-no)
Mano means "hand" in Italian — and that says it all. It is the barista who ultimately owns espresso quality. The machine is a tool; it's the hands using the tool that make it sing. A good barista knows how espresso should look, taste, and feel in the mouth — the perfectly flowing shot should resemble warm honey pouring from a spoon. Once pulled, the barista must immediately serve or incorporate the shot, as flavor changes and crema dissipates with every passing moment.
The Fifth Element: Water
There is a fifth key element not starting with "M": water. If the water doesn't taste good, neither will the espresso. Fresh, filtered, odor-free water minimizes service calls and extends machine life — incorrect water chemistry can corrode equipment or cause mineral buildup (scaling) inside machines. Every store has an individualized water treatment system based on on-site quality tests.

Grind

Fresh beans are finely ground to the correct particle size immediately before brewing. For the Black Eagle & Mythos, stainless-steel flat burrs grind to a consistent size — the barista sets particle size by watching shot time and tasting. The grinder has stepless adjustments for micro-correction. For the Mastrena®, a computerized system monitors shot time and automatically adjusts grind size for optimum extraction.

A Short History of Espresso

The first steam espresso machine received a patent in Turin, Italy, in 1884 as "new steam machinery for the economic and instantaneous confection of coffee beverages." These early machines used a large steam boiler to force hot water through "pucks" of coffee — fast and prolific, but less elegant than today's espresso, often over-extracted and quite bitter, with fewer than two bars of pressure (modern machines use around five times as much).

Over the next century, espresso machine innovation created boilers that could generate the pressure needed for the beverage we now know. The innovation is ongoing — today espresso is made on many different machines, in different styles, but all follow the same essential process.

Chapter 4

The Craft of Latte Art

Expertly steamed milk and quality espresso become your creative tools.

Espresso culture was born in Italy. As love for espresso expanded around the world, skilled and curious baristas began to experiment with how they poured steamed milk — noticing that with practice, they could craft designs out of creamy foam. The first beverage decorated with the iconic rosetta design was poured at Espresso Vivace in Seattle in 1992.

When a customer sees a barista mark a cap of foam with a handcrafted design, they see the high level of care being brought to their beverage. More than ever, customers love sharing pictures of their ornately decorated cups. With the rise of social media, #latteart is inspiring baristas around the globe to master new designs.

It Begins with Milk

Latte art is made possible only with correctly steamed and textured milk. Milk is made up of a complex mix of three components: sugars, fats, and proteins. Each plays an important role in milk steaming.

🍬
Sugar — The Source of Sweetness
▾
The primary source of milk's natural sweetness is lactose. Temperature affects taste — the human tongue is more sensitive to sweetness when food or drink is warm. When milk is heated during steaming, the lactose tastes sweeter. But if milk gets too hot, lactose interacts with other components, loses sweetness, and creates unpleasant flavor compounds associated with scalding. The ideal steaming temperature for adults is 150°–170°F (66°–77°C).
🧴
Fat — The Source of Creaminess
▾
Fat is the source of milk's naturally rich creaminess. Whole milk used in stores is about 4.5% milk fat; nonfat milk is 0%. Fat helps stabilize the structure of microfoam — the creamy, pourable foam necessary for latte art. A microfoam made with whole milk will be creamier and more luscious, while nonfat milk creates a slightly drier, stiffer foam. Both can be used to create latte art with practice.
🧬
Protein — The Structure Builder
▾
The caseins and whey proteins in milk play crucial roles in steaming. As air is introduced through the steam wand, the milk's proteins create a thin film around the surface of the bubbles, forming the structure needed for foam. These same proteins make whipped cream and cold foam possible. Different milks have different protein contents, affecting each milk's ability to maintain velvety microfoam.
The Milk Steaming Routine
1

Into the Pitcher — Start with Cold Milk

Pour the correct volume of cold milk for the beverage. Starting with cold milk gives the barista more time to reach the perfect aeration level before the milk gets too hot. Too much milk → under-aeration to avoid overflow. Too little → over-aeration to compensate.

2

Aerate First

Introduce air into the cold milk ("stretching") before steaming to final temperature. Aerating must happen below 100°F (38°C) — once proteins denature above this point, they can no longer smoothly combine into foam. A good gauge: when the pitcher feels similar in temperature to your palm (~98.6°F), stop aerating. Listen for the sound of ripping paper as a sign of proper aeration.

3

Correct Steam Wand Placement

Place the tip of the steam wand off-center and just under the surface of the milk. Too high → big useless bubbles. Too low → simply hot milk with no foam. After aeration, guide the wand deeper to create a whirlpool motion — this keeps small bubbles evenly incorporated, creating the glossy, wet-paint texture needed for latte art.

4

Steam to Temperature, Then Pour

Complete steaming to 150°–170°F. Swirl the pitcher to mix milk and foam. The glossy, smooth microfoam is buoyant — it floats gracefully on top of the espresso. This buoyancy is what allows a latte or cappuccino to have that beautiful crown of foam.

Troubleshooting Milk Texture
Under-Aerated Milk
Problem

Problem: Watery texture that will not hold a latte art design. Milk does not have quality microfoam.

Solution: Follow the Milk Steaming Routine. During aeration, listen for the sound of ripping paper and make sure the volume of milk grows. If aeration isn't happening correctly, try adjusting the steam wand tip to be closer to the surface.

Over-Aerated Milk
Problem

Problem: Large bubbles, lacks shine. Too stiff to pour latte art because too much air has been introduced.

Solution: During aeration, listen for the sound of ripping paper — you might be aerating too long. If large bubbles are blown in during aeration, the steam wand tip might not be fully submerged. Adjust it to be slightly deeper.

Separated Milk
Problem

Problem: Although milk was correctly textured, as you go to pour, steamed milk comes out first, followed by stiff foam. The microfoam has separated from the milk.

Solution: Even correctly textured milk will separate over time as foam rises. Groom your milk by swirling the pitcher to mix the milk and foam to maintain a smooth, velvety texture. Pour promptly after steaming.

Latte Art Designs

Master in this order: dot → heart → tulip → rosetta. Tap a design to see the steps.

🤍
Heart
Foundation
1
From a distance, pour a thin stream into the deepest part of the espresso until cup is half full.
2
Bring the pitcher close to the surface. Increase flow to lay a wide dot of foam in the center — this is your heart base.
3
As the cup nears full, tilt the spout back and draw a thin stream straight through the center of the dot, pulling it into a heart shape.
🌷
Tulip
Intermediate
1
From a distance, pour until cup is half to two-thirds full.
2
Bring pitcher close. Increase flow to lay a dot of foam in the center. Tilt pitcher away, cleanly breaking the stream.
3
Repeat — pour another dot, pushing the next layer into the first, shaping the tulip. Add additional layers as desired.
4
On your last layer, draw a pencil-thin stream straight through the design toward the cup's edge — pulling the dot into a beautiful stacked tulip.
🌿
Rosetta
Advanced
1
From a distance, pour until cup is half to two-thirds full.
2
Bring pitcher close. Increase flow and begin a side-to-side motion — this creates the rippled base of the rosetta.
3
Move the pitcher backwards while continuing the side-to-side motion — this forms the leaves of the rosetta.
4
Once at the back of the cup, hold position and lay foam to create the top heart of the rosetta.
5
Draw a pencil-thin stream straight through the design toward the cup's edge — this draws the final rosetta line.
Common Latte Art Problems
🔬
Design Is Too Small
▾
Problem: Good contrast but very small design.
Solution: Begin laying white foam earlier — bring the pitcher close to the surface sooner so the design has more time and milk to grow and fill the cup.
📐
Design Is Off-Center
▾
Problem: Good contrast but design is to one side.
Solution: The location of the pitcher and stream of milk determines where the design will be. For a centered, symmetrical design, keep the stream of milk in the center of the cup.
👻
Vanishing Latte Art
▾
Problem: White latte art vanishes as the cup fills.
Solution: Pouring from a distance pulls white foam down under the surface. Keep the spout as close to the surface as possible. If tilting the cup, move the pitcher and cup together as the beverage fills — flattening the cup without moving the pitcher increases distance and causes designs to shrink and vanish.
💨
Ghosting & Poor Contrast
▾
Problem: Beige splotches instead of crisp white-on-brown contrast.
Solution: If you hit the side of the cup or pour too aggressively, milk splashes and causes "ghosts." Pour a thin stream of textured milk into the deepest part of the espresso (the "pin drop") so milk cleanly dives under the surface. To correct existing ghosting, pour a thin stream in a spiral motion over the ghosted areas to pull the splotches back under the espresso.
Chapter 5

Immersed in Brewing

The power to bring it all to life is in your hands — literally.

Before a coffee reaches your store, time and effort have been invested all along the supply chain — farmers, mill workers, quality teams, roasters, and blenders. But one more pair of hands is needed to bring to full life all the beans' quality and rich flavor: yours.

This transformation is a unique responsibility of the barista. Unlike a winemaker who curates flavor from vine to bottle, it is the job of the barista to take what the master roasters and blenders envisioned and — with your own expertise — work that final magic. And it must be done right.

What Is Extraction?

Inside each coffee bean there are many different flavor and aromatic compounds. These are drawn out at different times in the brewing process — some pleasing, others not. This process is called extraction. Coffee is chemically complex — around 900 chemicals are extracted during brewing, and technically about 30% of the roasted bean is water-soluble. But not all that can be extracted should be.

Brewing Variables

Each brewing variable affects how much extraction occurs and what ends up in your cup. Tap to explore.

⚖️
Proportion / Brew Ratio
▾
Using the correct proportion of water to coffee is one of the Four Fundamentals of brewing. Starbucks recommends a brew ratio of 18:1 (water-to-coffee) — calculated as 6 fl oz of water per 2 tablespoon scoop of coffee. The lower the ratio, the stronger the beverage; the higher the ratio, the weaker. Changing the ratio affects both strength and extraction. Across the industry, ratios of 16:1 to 18:1 produce a pleasant strength agreeable to most customers.
⚙️
The Grind
▾
Grinding increases the surface area of coffee, allowing water to pass through the beans and pull out flavor compounds. Finer grind → more surface area → faster extraction. Coarser grind → less surface area → slower extraction. There is no universally ideal grind size — the key is choosing the right grind for your brewing method. Ground coffee oxidizes quickly, so always grind immediately before brewing. A coffee bean's surface area increases more than 10,000 times when finely ground for espresso — no wonder espresso can extract in about 20 seconds!
🌡️
Temperature
▾
Coffee extraction happens at different rates given different water temperatures. Too hot → extraction happens very quickly. Cold water → very slow extraction. This is why cold brew takes ~20 hours using a coarse grind, while a hot press takes only 4 minutes with the same grind. Starbucks stores use filtered water at the precisely correct temperature — every single brew.
⏱️
Time (Contact Time)
▾
As long as coffee is in contact with water, extraction is underway. In a coffee press, the barista controls contact time by plunging. In a pour-over, time is controlled by gravity, filter type, and grind size resistance. The brewing recipe specifies exactly how long to brew for optimal extraction.
🌀
Turbulence
▾
Turbulence is the mixing action created as water passes over and through coffee. It can be caused by movement from a brew head, kettle, or manual agitation. Turbulence increases extraction — too much can lead to over-extraction. When using a pour-over, pouring too quickly causes excess agitation; too slowly can cause drips and splashes. The recipe guides you to pour a steady, even stream that generates just the right amount of turbulence.
The Three Key Brewing Ingredients
🫘
Coffee — Just the Right Degree of Fresh
▾
To brew a great cup, start with fresh-tasting beans. Stale coffee has lost much of its aroma and acidity — volatile compounds degrade over time, leaving dull, cardboard-like flavors. Starbucks recommends using coffee within one week of opening its FlavorLock™ bag. However, coffee that is too fresh (just roasted) is also problematic — the naturally built-up gases rush out when wetted, making it difficult to extract the right flavor compounds. Wait one week after roasting before brewing.
💧
Water — The Perfect, Good-Tasting Balance
▾
A cup of coffee is about 98% water. Water with too many minerals makes it harder to dissolve coffee. Distilled water (all minerals removed) causes very quick extraction that over-extracts negative compounds. Every Starbucks store has a specialized water-filtration system that removes undesirable compounds while ensuring only the right concentration of the right compounds remains — so no matter what's in your city's water, it's optimized for brewing by the time it reaches your cup.
📄
The Filter — Flavor Clarity & Mouthfeel
▾
Metal filters (coffee press, espresso, Clover®) let through more solids and coffee oils → more robust mouthfeel. Paper filters (batch brew, pour-over, siphon) trap more solids and oils → lighter body, cleaner mouthfeel. Both soluble and insoluble compounds are extracted. The type of filter directly shapes the texture of the finished beverage — use the best filter for your brew method and desired cup quality.
Extraction & Strength

Extraction controls flavor — what flavor compounds are in the cup. Strength controls intensity — how much of those flavors there are, as a ratio of dissolved coffee to water. A typical cup of brewed coffee is only about 1.2% coffee / 98.8% water. A shot of espresso is about 10% coffee / 90% water.

The goal of brewing is to achieve a pleasing balance of both. To reduce strength without altering extraction, add hot water after brewing — this is exactly what an Americano is.

Though you will be grinding and brewing some of the greatest arabica beans in the world, even the best coffee will not taste right if it's brewed incorrectly. Do the coffee justice — and all the people along the way who made it possible. The power is in your hands.

— Starbucks Coffee Academy, Level 300
Equipment Care & Maintenance
Why It Matters
It does not take long for equipment to experience a buildup of coffee oils. These oils can make a perfectly pulled espresso shot taste rancid, or render an otherwise well-brewed cup undrinkable. The average cost of a service call on an espresso machine equals approximately 333 lattes! Preventative maintenance — regularly scheduled visits to replace parts that experience wear — ensures a long service life and protects beverage quality.
